Tunable ferromagnetic ordering in MoS2 nanosheets with fluorine adsorption †

Daqiang Gao , Shoupeng Shi , Kun Tao , Baorui Xia and Desheng Xue *
Key Laboratory for Magnetism and Magnetic Materials of MOE, Key Laboratory of Special Function Materials and Structure Design, Ministry of Education, Lanzhou University, Lanzhou 730000, P. R. China. E-mail: xueds@lzu.edu.cn; Fax: +86 0931 8914160; Tel: +86 0931 8914160

Received 20th January 2015 , Accepted 29th January 2015

First published on 2nd February 2015

Two-dimensional ferromagnetic ultrathin nanosheets hold great promise for next generation electronics and spintronics. Here, intrinsic ferromagnetism was achieved through a new effective strategy by fluorine adsorption on MoS2 nanosheets, where the fluorinated MoS2 nanosheets exhibit stable ferromagnetic hysteresis at room temperature with saturation magnetization of 0.06 emu g−1 and magnetoresistance of 4.1%. The observed ferromagnetism can be tuned by changing the concentration of the adatom. On the basis of first-principle calculations, it is shown that not only fluorine absorbed MoS2 monolayer favours spontaneous spin polarization and local moment formation, but also that the spin moments can exhibit long range magnetic ordering. This work paves a new pathway to engineer the magnetic properties of the two-dimensional nano-materials.

Two-dimensional (2D) crystals of transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) have received significant attention due to their potential use in a wide range of novel applications as well as in basic research.1–3 As a member of the TMD family, molybdenum disulfide (MoS2) isolated in the single-layer form was the first to attract attention, demonstrating a high on/off current ratio and low off-state current.4 Recently, integrated circuits based on single-layer MoS2, bilayer MoS2, signal amplifiers, and nonvolatile memory cells were also demonstrated.5–7 Moreover, the presence of a direct band gap in MoS2 due to the dimensional confinement makes it interesting for applications in optoelectronics.8–10

In addition, studies on the magnetic response of MoS2 were also proposed recently, proving its potential applications in spintronics and nanoelectronic devices. Many theoretical studies have suggested that, although bulk MoS2 is a diamagnetic material like graphite, MoS2 becomes ferromagnetic when MoS2 nanoribbons are formed with zigzag edges, or when defects are induced.11,12 Experimentally, ferromagnetism was obtained in freestanding nanosheets,13 in vertically aligned nanosheets,14 and in bulk MoS2 irradiated by protons,15 where the existence of ferromagnetism was partly attributed to the presence of zigzag edge or defect states at the grain boundaries. However, for its use in nanoelectronics, the magnetic coupling modulated by the edge and defect states in MoS2 nanosheets is always somewhat rough as compared to the ideal requirement (stable and precision magnetic spin ordering) and, thus, is difficult to control in the actual application, which hampers the development of this material in nanoelectronic devices.16

Previous studies have found that the absorption of a non-metal element on the surface of low-dimensional systems can induce local magnetic moment.17,18 Compared with the magnetic moment of d or f electrons of transition-metal or rare-earth atoms, the magnetism based on the sp states of the non-metal element has some obvious advantages, such as stronger long-range exchange coupling interactions and no clustering of magnetic ions. Fluorination was confirmed to be the most effective route to introduce localized spins in graphene and it has been reported that fluorination can induce tunable magnetism in the BN nanotubes and nanosheets.19,20 Recently, previous reports on the possible magnetic properties of fluorinated TMDs have also been demonstrated theoretically. For example, He et al. investigated the adsorption of nonmetal atoms on pristine MoS2 and found that H, B, C, N and F atoms can induce local magnetic moments.21 Yue et al. studied the magnetic and electronic properties of MoS2 sheets by substituting H, B, C, O, N and F atoms with sulfur defects (VS). The calculated binding energies show that all the substitutional atoms are strongly bound to VS, resulting in different magnetic behaviors.22 These anticipated results open a new way to modify the magnetic properties of the monolayer MoS2. However, by examining the magnetic properties of non-metal atoms, absorbed MoS2 could not be realized experimentally. Therefore, it is of crucial importance to develop a feasible method for synthesizing and exploring their corresponding magnetic properties.

In this paper, we have reported the preparation and corresponding magnetic properties of the fluorinated MoS2 (F-MoS2) nanosheets. First-principles calculation results demonstrate that the F atom can chemically absorb on the MoS2 monolayer favouring the top of the S atom, where the total magnetic moment of 1.0μB was found in the F-absorbed MoS2 monolayer. Magnetic measurement results indicate that the enhancement of the room temperature ferromagnetism was observed for F-MoS2 nanosheets compared to the primitive nanosheets, which is quite promising for applications in spintronic devices.

Results and discussion

F-MoS2 nanosheets were prepared by a two-step method as shown in Fig. 1a. In brief, ultrathin MoS2 nanosheets were first obtained by exfoliating the bulk form in N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF, 100 mL) solution as per our previous report.13 Then, 1.0 g of the exfoliated MoS2 nanosheets and 2.0 g ammonium fluoride (NH4F) were further ground into a homogeneous form in deionized water and transferred into a Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave, which was heated at 180 °C for 24 h and then cooled to room temperature. The autoclave container was taken out and the product was centrifuged and washed with ethanol and deionized water repeatedly. Finally, the as-obtained sample was dried in a vacuum oven for 24 h. Subsequently, systematic characterizations were performed to verify that the final products were ultrathin nanosheet structures. A transmission electron microscopy (TEM, TecnaiTM G2 F30, FEI, USA) image of F-MoS2 nanosheets shown in Fig. 1b revealed that the lateral diameters of the nanosheets are ∼200 nm. Results indicate that the colloidal dispersions of the F-MoS2 nanosheets are highly stable over a period of several days and display a typical Tyndall effect, reasonably indicating the formation of freestanding and homogeneous 2D ultrathin sheets (inset in Fig. 1b). Further high resolution TEM results for the selected regions of one nanosheet are shown in Fig. 1c, indicating that the nanosheets are crystallographically good. The thickness of the products was measured by atomic force microscopy (AFM Dimension 3100 with Nanoscope IIIa controller, Veeco, CA, USA). As can be seen from Fig. 1d, the thicknesses of ultrathin nanosheets are ranging from 2.9 nm to 3.4 nm, which indicates that the ultrathin nanosheets are composed of 3–5 atomic monolayers.
Fig. 1 (a) Schematic illustration for the synthesis of fluorinated-MoS2 nanosheets. (b) TEM and the corresponding colloidal DMF dispersion displaying the Tyndall effect. (c) HRTEM and (d) AFM image and the corresponding height profile for the fluorinated-MoS2 nanosheets.

A typical X-ray diffraction (XRD, X’ Pert PRO PHILIPS with Cu Kα radiation) spectrum of the F-MoS2 nanosheets is shown in Fig. 2a; it is possible to assign the reflection peaks to the family of lattice planes of hexagonal MoS2 (JCPDS Card no.77-1716). XRD results indicate that there is no other new phase introduced into the fluorination process. Moreover, the bonding characteristics and the composition of the exfoliated MoS2 samples were captured by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, Kratos Axis Ultra). Besides the element of C, wide XPS spectra of the F-MoS2 sample show only signals arising from elements Mo, S and F. The peaks corresponding to the S 2p1/2 and S 2p3/2 orbitals of divalent sulfide ions (S2−), are observed at 163.3 and 162.0 eV, shown in Fig. 2b. The Mo 3d XPS spectrum of F-MoS2 nanosheets, reported in Fig. 2c shows two strong peaks at 229.3 and 232.5 eV, respectively, attributed to the doublet Mo 3d5/2 and Mo 3d3/2. Besides, the peak at the binding energy of 686.9 eV is attributed to the F 1s spectra. All these results are consistent with the reported values.23 Elemental mapping results shown in Fig. 2f–i also give the information that the samples contain elements Mo, S and F, where the elemental distribution is uniform.


Fig. 2 (a) XRD, (b)–(d) XPS results, and (e)–(j) TEM images and the elemental mapping for the fluorinated-MoS2 nanosheets.

The magnetic properties of the F-MoS2 nanosheets were studied by using a superconducting quantum interferometer device (SQUID) magnetometer from Quantum Design. Fig. 3a shows the magnetization curves (M–H) of the F-MoS2 nanosheets measured at different temperatures (10–300 K), and the magnetization curve for the exfoliated MoS2 nanosheets is also given to compare, where the diamagnetic background has been subtracted (the primitive curves are shown in Fig. S1†). It can be seen that the exfoliated MoS2 nanosheets show the saturation magnetization (Ms) of 0.001 emu g−1 at 300 K, which corresponds to our previous results, where ferromagnetism is considered to be related to the edge states.13 However, it is interesting that the sample's Ms shows great enhancement after fluorination. As shown in Fig. 3a, the F-MoS2 nanosheets have Ms of 0.07 and 0.06 emu g−1 at 10 and 300 K, respectively. The magnetic hysteresis loops measured in the low field range are plotted in Fig. S2.† Obviously, ferromagnetic behaviour of the F-MoS2 is robust at this temperature range with the remanent magnetic moment (Mr) and coercivity (Hc) decreasing with the increase in temperature. In addition, the magnetization dependent temperature (M–T) curves of the F-MoS2 nanosheets presented in Fig. 3b provide further evidence to prove that the sample intrinsically exhibits room temperature ferromagnetism. Obviously, zero field cooling (ZFC) and field cooling (FC) curves showed distinct difference in a wide temperature range from 10 up to 300 K, revealing that the Curie temperature is higher than 300 K. Most importantly, there is no block temperature appearance in the ZFC curve, which clearly reveals that there are no ferromagnetic clusters in our sample and provides evidence for ferromagnetism in the samples.24 Besides, it can be seen that there is a magnetic moment change at about 120 K in the FC curve. Such a magnetic anomaly was found when antiferromagnetic (AFM) correlations competed with ferromagnetic (FM) interactions. This result was similar to that obtained for graphene,25,26 somewhat like spin glass behavior in a frustrated system. Though the origin remained unclear, a coexistence of ferromagnetism and antiferromagnetism was well accepted for a biparticle system.27 Magnetic measurement results indicate that the raw materials (h-MoS2 powder and NH4F) show diamagnetism only (see Fig. S3†). Thus, we conclude that the enhanced ferromagnetism observed in the F-MoS2 nanosheets is mainly induced by fluorination. Of note, the ferromagnetic impurities such as Fe, Co, Ni could also be excluded by inductively coupled plasma (ICP) results, whose contents are all below 10 ppm. Therefore, all of the above analyses confirm that the room temperature ferromagnetism in F-MoS2 nanosheets is due to the inherent nature.28,29

By using the SQUID and vibrating sample magnetometer equipped with a high temperature chamber (VSM, VSM Model EV9, MicroSense, LLC), we could obtain the information of Ms dependence on temperature for the F-MoS2 nanosheets. As shown in Fig. 3c, the data can be well fitted by the following equation which is common for a ferromagnetic material:30,31M(T) = A(1 − T/TC)β, where A is a coefficient related to the spontaneous magnetization, TC is the ferromagnetic Curie temperature, and β is the critical exponent. The best fitting curve (T < TC) is shown by a red curve, where the fitted sample's Curie temperature is about 845 K. Such a high TC ferromagnetism in few-layered F-MoS2 nanosheets makes them promising candidates for application in spintronic devices.32,33 Besides, we also studied the effect of the amount of fluoride on the magnetic properties of the F-MoS2 nanosheets.

We changed the quantity of NH4F from 2.0 g to 1.5 g and 0.5 g, respectively, during the experiment, keeping the other conditions constant, where the concentration of fluorine atoms were 1.3%, 3.7% and 7.0% with the F− electron state for the three samples (NH4F = 0.5 g, 1.0 g and 2.0 g), which were obtained by XPS measurements. As can be seen from Fig. 3d, the F-MoS2 nanosheets show different Ms, indicating that the ferromagnetism of the samples can be tuned by changing the concentration of the adsorption atoms.

The above results indicate that the observed larger ferromagnetism in F-MoS2 nanosheets is mainly induced by introducing the F atom absorbed on the MoS2 nanosheets. To further understand how fluoride influences the ferromagnetism in MoS2 nanosheets, the geometries, electronic structures, and magnetic behaviour of F-absorbed MoS2 monolayers were investigated by using first principles calculations. The studied model consists of a 4 × 4 × 1 MoS2 supercell (including 32 S and 16 Mo atoms) and an adatom (F atom). The distance between two adjacent MoS2 monolayers is larger than 16 Å to avoid the layer–layer interaction. Calculation results indicate that the most energetically favorable absorbed position is at the top of the S atom for the adatom. The spin-polarized calculations with density functional theory formalism were performed using the Vienna ab initio simulation package (VASP) with the generalized gradient approximation of Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof functional.34 The plane wave basis set with a kinetic energy cutoff of 500 eV was employed. The sampling of the Brillouin zone was done using a 5 × 5 × 1 Monkhorst–Pack grid. All the structures were fully relaxed until the Hellmann–Feynman force was less than 0.01 eV Å−1.35

As shown in Fig. 4a, a significant asymmetry between the spin-up state and spin-down state in the density of states (DOS) near the Fermi level suggests the intrinsic ferromagnetism of an F-absorbed MoS2 monolayer. As is well known, the value of the Curie temperature depends on the exchange stiffness and the DOS at the Fermi level. The calculated DOS result display obvious spin splitting at the conduction band and valence band, which means that the relatively high Curie temperature in this configuration is identical to our experimental results. The total magnetic moments of this electronic structure is about 1.0μB. The spin-resolved DOS projected on the 4d orbital of the nearest Mo atom, p orbitals of the adatom and the nearest S atom are also presented in Fig. 4b. All of them show a significant asymmetry between the spin-up state and spin-down state near the Fermi level, illustrating both of them contribute the magnetic moment to the total magnetic moments. Obviously, the PDOS results clearly uncover that the 2p orbital of the adatom are localized, spin-polarized, and consequently induce polarizations of S 3p and Mo 4d electrons near the adatoms. Near the Fermi energy, the spin-up states are fully occupied and the spin-down states are either unoccupied or partially occupied, which is the origin of magnetic moment for the F-adsorbed MoS2 monolayer. These results are consistent with the spin resolved charge density distribution of the F-absorbed MoS2 monolayers as shown in Fig. 4c. It is worth noting that the overlaps between F 2p states and 3s states of the second-nearest S, 4d states of the third-nearest Mo atoms are still evident, which corresponds to the large spatial extension of the spin density of the F-MoS2 monolayer.

(c) The spin resolved charge density isosurface (0.001 Å−3) of the F-absorbed MoS2 monolayers. Mo atoms are indicated by blue balls, S atoms by yellow balls and F atoms by red balls, respectively.

To further study the magnetic coupling between the fluorination induced local magnetic moments, we adopted large-size supercells containing 32 primitive cells (denoted as 8 × 4 × 1) and two F ions, which correspond to the fluorination rate of 1/16 in a MoS2 monolayer. Starting from different initial spin arrangements, self-consistent calculations lead to two types of magnetic orderings. One has the local magnetic moments aligned in an “FM” way. Another has the local magnetic moments aligned in an “AFM” way.36,37 Calculation results indicate that two stable magnetic structures were obtained: one was FM (EAFM − EFM = 0.32 meV) and the other was AFM (EAFM − EFM = −0.48 meV). It can be seen from Fig. 5a that most of the spin polarized states are localized around the F dopants and led to the formation of the local magnetic moment while the long range magnetic coupling was mediated between the two moments. However, as the distance between two F dopants decreased, the system would be more stable in the AFM phase, due to the broadening of the dopant band being equal to the spin splitting, caused by a larger degree of overlap between the unpaired electrons (Fig. 5b). These results are similar to the reports of C doped BN and defective C60 systems.36,38 To sum up, the above calculation results indicate that the spin moments induced by topological fluorine adsorption on MoS2 nanosheets can be (anti)ferromagnetically coupled to exhibit long rang magnetic ordering, which corresponds to the experimental results.


Fig. 5 Spatial distribution of the spin-polarization density of the MoS2 monolayers with two F atoms absorbed. (a) Ferromagnetism coupling, (b) antiferromagnetism coupling. The unit is Å−3, Mo atoms are indicated by blue balls, S atoms by yellow balls and F atoms by red balls, respectively.

In order to check the intrinsic nature of the ferromagnetism in F-MoS2 nanosheets and its possible applications in spintronics, we measured the magnetoresistance (MR) of F-MoS2 nanosheets by the two-terminal method, where the F-MoS2 nanosheets were pressed into a 5 mm-diameter piece. The MR results for the samples with different F contents are shown in Fig. 6 in the field range of −8000 Oe < H < 8000 Oe, where the MR value is defined as [R(H) − R(0)]/R(0) and R(0) is the resistance at zero field. It can be seen that all the samples show negative hysteresis MR curves, where the sample with the F content of 7.0% shows the max MR value of 4.1%. Similar negative hysteresis MR has been observed in reduced graphene oxide and VS2 nanosheets, which is a clear indication of the intrinsic nature of the observed ferromagnetism.39,40

In addition, we have quantitatively analyzed the magnetization signal to obtain the magnetic moment per absorbed F atom. Results indicate that the F concentration for sample F-MoS2-2.0 is about 7.0%. Therefore, with an F/Mo + S + F ratio of 7%, the number of F dopants per unit mass, is given by NC = (NA/MR) × 7% = 2.63 × 1020 dopants g−1,36 where MR is the relative molecular weight of MoS2 (160.06) and NA is the Avogadro's constant of 6.02 × 1023. The value of Ms for the F-MoS2 nanosheets was 0.07 emu g−1 as shown in Fig. 3a, and, thus, the magnetic moment per absorbed F is given by μC = Ms/NC = 1.16 × 10−22 emu = 1.25 × 10−2μB. Comparing with our theoretical value of 1.0μB per F dopant, only 1/8 F dopants seemed to contribute to the ferromagnetism in the experiment. It is noteworthy that we used a MoS2 monolayer model in these calculations, for the synthesized F-MoS2 nanosheets which contain multi-layers; the fluorination rate will be lower than this model. In addition, the reason may be that some absorbed F atoms formed islands on the surface of MoS2 nanosheets. Apart from these, some AFM couplings also make the total magnetic moment from these two F dopants to be zero. Therefore, only those F dopants that have no counterparts on the vicinal site sub-lattice would contribute to the total magnetic moment of F-MoS2 nanosheets in our case. However, to what extent each factor will play a role is still a question and our work is on the way.

Conclusions

In summary, we have demonstrated that the involvement of F atoms absorbed on MoS2 nanosheets can induce intrinsic ferromagnetism, which is verified by both magnetic characterizations and theoretical calculations. The saturation magnetization of F-MoS2 nanosheets at room temperature was as high as 0.06 emu g−1, which is larger than that of the exfoliated MoS2 nanosheets caused by the defect and edge states. Moreover, negative hysteresis MR curves can be observed for all the samples, revealing its possible applications in spintronics. This finding broadens our horizon and it is anticipated that the introduction of a non-magnetic absorption strategy could be an effective way for engineering the intrinsic magnetic properties of 2D nanomaterials.
