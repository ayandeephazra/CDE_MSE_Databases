Dynamic magnetic MOFs†

Eugenio Coronado * and Guillermo Mínguez Espallargas *

Received 23rd July 2012

First published on 12th November 2012

In this review we combine the use of coordination chemistry with the concepts of molecular magnetism to design magnetic Metal–Organic Frameworks (MOFs) in which the crystalline network undergoes a dynamic change upon application of an external stimulus. The various approaches so far developed to prepare these kinds of chemically or physically responsive MOFs with tunable magnetic properties are presented.

1. Introduction

Stimuli-responsive materials for which physical or chemical properties can be tuned by applying an external stimulus are attracting considerable interest in view of their potential applications as chemical switches, memories or molecular sensors.1–8 These kinds of materials may be viewed as a new generation of multifunctional materials in which two (or more) functional properties are not simply co-existing in the same material, but strongly coupled to guarantee its response to the external stimulus.9,10

A molecular-based building block approach has provided appealing examples of such systems, in particular at the single-molecule level, as is the case of bistable catenanes and rotaxanes.11–13 These interlocked molecules are able to perform controlled mechanical motions in solution and therefore they have been proposed to be an ideal platform for the design of molecular nanodevices displaying mechanical, electronic information or sensing functions. However, these applications in molecular electronics are strongly limited by the need to interface these functional molecules with the macroscopic world. Thus, an active focus of research in this area involves the organization of these molecules on surfaces, at interfaces or in solid matrices.14

Another source of stimuli-driven molecular materials results from the construction of crystalline coordination frameworks formed by molecular units capable of undergoing a motion in the solid state. The structural versatility of coordination chemistry has allowed the engineering of materials having novel topologies and remarkable properties. One can, for example, introduce flexibility into solids by taking advantage of the structural flexibility of the ligand,15–17 or from the ability of the coordination site to accept, release or exchange coordinating molecules. These processes can induce structural changes in the coordination framework. The result will be a dynamic material that undergoes reversible structural changes to respond to the applied stimulus.18 However, these dynamic materials are not restricted to the use of flexible ligands or changes in the coordination sphere. Thus, one can induce a change in the electronic/magnetic properties of a system by inducing a controlled movement (a rotation, for example) of an asymmetric bridging ligand connecting two different metal sites, as that may cause a change in the crystal field around these coordination sites and, hence, in their electronic configurations (from high to low spin, for example).19

In this review we will focus on these extended crystalline materials to illustrate with different examples the various possibilities provided by coordination chemistry to design responsive dynamic materials. In particular, we will concentrate on those materials having magnetism as the tunable property with chemical and physical stimuli. We will present the effects on the magnetic properties of chemi- or physisorption of molecules as chemical stimuli, and light, pressure or temperature as physical stimuli, currently topics of great interest in molecular magnetism. So far, the research in this area has been mainly focused on the synthesis of new multifunctional materials combining magnetism with a second property (electrical conductivity or superconductivity, molecular bistablity,…).20–25 However, a more recent application of these materials concerns the nascent area of molecular spintronics,26,27 where responsive magnetic molecular materials, such as photomagnets,28 magneto-resistive materials,29 or multiferroic materials30,31 are very promising. However, all these magnetic materials are based on rigid lattices. Hence, they do not take advantage of the structural flexibility induced by the presence of either constituent or inserted molecules. A major source for these dynamic materials is provided by the so-called Metal–Organic Frameworks (MOFs). In general, these coordination polymers are composed of metal ions or metal clusters as nodes and multitopic organic ligands as linkers.32–36 They have potential applications that range from catalysis and separations of mixtures, to gas storage media for sensors and electronic devices.37–40 The most common strategy for the synthesis of MOFs involves the use of rigid ligands to bridge between the metal nodes leading to robust and rigid frameworks (static MOFs). However, as has been pointed above, there is growing interest in the search for dynamic MOFs. We will show in this review that magnetic MOFs are particularly sensitive to the structural changes provoked by an external stimulus.

The first part of this review presents dynamic magnetic MOFs which can be modified with a chemical stimulus, typically guest uptake or guest incorporation. The major part of this section deals with porous MOFs, where the presence of channels facilitates the entrance of the guest molecule. However, it is also shown that the presence of pores is not a requirement for chemisorption to take place. The second part of the review presents dynamic MOFs where a physical stimulus induces changes in the bridging ligands, and hence, in the magnetic properties. The examples here presented are intended to be crystal-to-crystal transformations, although it must be noted that some of these could also be a solvent mediated process, i.e. a microscopic recrystallization, analogous to that demonstrated for some anion exchange reactions involving crystalline solids.41,42

2. Chemically-driven dynamic MOFs

2.1. Porous MOFs

With the recent explosion in research on MOFs,43–56 the use of a chemical stimulus such as gas sorption to affect the magnetic properties of a porous coordination polymer has been extensively explored.9,57–60 The initial focus concentrated on the molecule-based magnets which show magnetic cooperativity at low temperatures. Still, this approach is not very practical for sensing applications as the sorption of molecules takes place at relatively high temperatures, while the magnetic ordering occurs at very low temperature. Spin-crossover complexes have been used to overcome this difficulty in combining magnetic cooperativity and porosity, as in this last case magnetic cooperativity is not needed. In fact, the spin-crossover phenomenon can occur near room temperature, being these paramagnetic complexes extremely sensitive to subtle structural changes in metal coordination environments.61–63 In the following sections we will illustrate with some relevant examples the possibilities provided by these two approaches.
2.1.1. Porous magnets. The sorption/desorption of guest molecules in porous magnets can be used to tune the magnetism due to the sensitivity of the exchange interactions to subtle structural changes. These structural changes can involve either the bridging ligands or, directly, the coordination environment around the metal ions. Dehydration processes have long been used to change the coordination environment and the dimensionality of a hydrated coordination polymer, thus provoking significant changes in the magnetic properties.64,65 However, this process is often irreversible or produces a collapse in the structure when the solvent is removed.66,67 Reversibility of such structural changes has been successfully achieved only with the use of MOFs. We should mention in this context Kahn's pioneering work on “molecular magnetic sponges”, where changes in the magnetic properties occurred upon sorption of water.68 However, the lack of structural data after the transformation limits the information on the changes that take place in both the metal centres and the ligands. This situation is similar in other recent examples of chiral magnets with important changes in the magnetic properties (a shift of 21 K in TC) upon reversible dehydration.69
Changes affecting the ligand. Small changes in the geometry of the bridging ligand are sufficient to affect the magnetic properties of a MOF. An example of this kind has been reported by Miyasaka and co-workers70 in the charge-transfer compound [{Ru2(O2CPh-o-Cl)4}2TCNQ(MeO)2]·CH2Cl2 (o-ClPhCO2− = o-chlorobenzoate; TCNQ(MeO)2 = 2,5-dimethoxy-7,7,8,8-tetracyanoquinodimethane) upon solvation/desolvation of the crystallization solvent. The structure consists of two-dimensional layers with interstitial CH2Cl2 molecules located in the void spaces between the layers, which can be lost at room temperature to form a dried sample (Fig. 1). Interestingly, the original material can be regenerated upon exposure to CH2Cl2 vapors for 72 h. Although the material exhibits a robust framework, this process produces a modification in the ordering/disordering of a pendant ligand that leads to a significant change in the magnetic ground state. Thus, the solvated compound possesses an antiferromagnetic ground state below TN = 75 K, which is altered to a ferromagnetic state with TC ≈ 56 K for the dried sample (Fig. 1). The magnetic change occurs as a result of slight structural fluctuations caused by the ordering/disordering of ligand orientation driven by the fix-and-release of the CH2Cl2 molecules acting as a clip.

Reversible changes in the ligand structure may also occur in the magnetic MOF [Cu(F-pymo)2(H2O)1.25]n (F-pymo = 5-fluoropyrimidin-2-olate) upon insertion of gas molecules in the pores. This compound, reported by Navarro, Parra, Sironi, and co-workers71 has helical channels of ca. 2.9 Å diameter which are filled with water molecules that can be removed upon heating (Fig. 2). The activated solid behaves as a molecular sieve and preferentially adsorbs H2 over N2, in addition to CO2. The as-synthesized solid shows antiferromagnetic interactions and it orders as a canted antiferromagnet below TN = 24 K. Such temperature is reduced to 22 K upon activation. Interestingly, the ordering temperature is increased to 29 K when the material is loaded with CO2 (Fig. 2), presumably due to the structural perturbation exerted by the CO2 guests, which affect the exchange interaction pathways through the ligands.

Changes affecting the coordination environment. Most of the known examples involving this kind of changes are related with the sorption/desorption of solvent molecules, most frequently water. In a first case an increase in the coordination number takes place when a solvent molecule is added.72 We can mention as example the three-dimensional coordination polymer [Co3(L)2(BTEC)] [HL = 3,5-di(imidazol-1-yl)benzoic acid, H4BTEC = 1,2,4,5-benzenetetracarboxylic acid] reported by Su et al.73 This MOF has 1D open channels (Fig. 3) that show selective adsorption of water molecules over N2, CH3OH, and CH3CH2OH. One of the CoII centres provides an open coordination site for the water molecule. Thus, upon exposure to air or water vapour or through the immersion into water at room temperature for several hours, the coordination geometry of this CoII centre changes from square pyramid to octahedron, producing a change in colour from dark-violet to red. Additional water molecules are located in the pores, resulting in the compound [Co3(L)2(BTEC)(H2O)2]·2H2O. This dynamic process also alters the dimensions of the pores, which are expanded as a consequence of the sorption of water. The adsorption of water molecules produces some modifications in the ferromagnetic coupling between the CoII ions within the trinuclear unit, which are promoted through the carboxylate groups. The decrease in magnetic coupling J upon hydration is consistent with the increase in the Co–Co distance, whereas the increase in D is due to the change of geometry.

A second case involves an exchange of guest molecules by adsorbed molecules which also produces the elimination of a coordinated solvent molecule, thus resulting in the decrease in the coordination number of the metal centre. As example we can mention the ferromagnetic porous coordination polymer Cu3[W(CN)8]2(pyrimidine)2·8H2O reported by Ohkoshi et al.74 In this MOF the magnetic properties can be tuned by the presence of n-propanol vapors (Fig. 4). This heterometallic coordination polymer is composed of alternated Cu and W ions linked by cyanide groups. The two independent Cu2+ ions present a pseudo-octahedral geometry with the coordination sphere completed either by pyrimidine ligands or water molecules. The two types of Cu centres result in two different Cu–NC–W exchange couplings, one ferromagnetic and one antiferromagnetic. The exposure of this MOF to vapours of n-propanol produces an exchange of the solvent molecules, yielding a material of formula Cu3[W(CN)8]2(pyrimidine)2·3/2PrOH·9/4H2O, where the non-coordinated water molecules have been replaced by propanol molecules. More interesting, however, is the elimination of one of the Cu-coordinated water molecules, yielding two distinct geometries: one Cu atom is 5-coordinated square pyramidal and the other Cu atom is a 6-coordinated pseudo-octahedron. This change of coordination of one Cu centre produces a change in the Cu–NC–W antiferromagnetic exchange coupling, whereas the ferromagnetic exchange coupling involving the 6-coordinate Cu centre remains the same. Thus, spontaneous magnetization is shifted from TC = 9.5 K to TC = 12.0 K, and an increase in the coercive field (from 30 G to 1000 G) is observed.

Another example of dynamic MOF involving exchange of molecules at the coordination site is provided by the series [Cu2M(tzdc)2(H2O)2]·2H2O [M = Fe2+ or Mn2+, tzdc3− = 1,2,3-triazole-4,5-dicarboxylate] reported by Chen and co-workers.75 These two compounds contain microporous layers with water molecules allocated in the pores. The tzdc3− ligand acts as a μ3-bridge between three metals and provides the magnetic exchange pathway of the system. These materials release both the coordinated and the non-coordinated water molecules upon heating, a process that is accompanied by a change in the octahedral coordination sphere of the Fe2+/Mn2+ atoms from O6 to O4N2. This modification produces a reversible change in the magnetism, in which the ferrimagnetic behaviour present in the original compound is suppressed (Fig. 5).

A similar situation is encountered in other coordination polymers based on cyanide or thiocyanide ligands, where the elimination of coordinated H2O molecules provokes a topochemical reaction with change of the coordination sphere from N4O2 to N6 with an increase in dimensionality from 1D to 2D76,77 or from N3N′O2 to N4N′ and N3N′78 with a reversible variation in Tc.

A third case involves the change in the coordination site from octahedral to tetrahedral, a situation that has been extensively encountered. An example has been reported by Zhang et al.79 in the chiral one-dimensional porous coordination polymer [FeIII(Tp)(CN)3]4FeII(H2O)2FeII (Tp = hydrotris(pyrazoly)borate). This compound behaves as a metamagnet at 2 K, with a field-induced transition from an antiferromagnetic to a ferrimagnetic state. It is formed by [FeIII(Tp)(CN)3]− units that act as bidentate ligands through two of its three cyanide groups in cis positions, which coordinate the FeII centres thus forming bimetallic double chains. Each FeII acts as bridge between four [FeIII(Tp)(CN)3]− units and two water molecules are coordinated to alternated centres, resulting in two different coordination modes for the FeII, octahedral and tetrahedral. Upon exposure to vapours of water and acetonitrile, the solvent molecules not only are sorbed in the voids, but also are inserted into some of the Fe–CN bonds causing a rupture of the chain and the formation of hexanuclear clusters of formula [{FeIII(Tp)(CN)3}4{FeII(MeCN)(H2O)2}2]·10H2O·2MeCN, in which the tetrahedral FeII sites have increased their coordination to octahedral. These hexanuclear units are antiferromagnetic and do not exhibit long range order. They are linked to each other to form a chain through hydrogen bonding interactions of the terminal CN− on the [Fe(Tp)(CN)3]− moiety and the weakly coordinated solvent molecules on FeII. Hence, upon solvation, the compound changes its magnetism from a metamagnet to an antiferromagnet, a process which is reversible upon heating at 150 °C.

A related example has recently been reported by Ohkoshi, Hashimoto, and co-workers.80 The compound is a Prussian-blue analogue of formula Co[Cr(CN)6]2/3·zH2O. This pink solid is a ferromagnet with a TC of 28 K that reversibly transforms into a blue antiferromagnet with a TC of 22 K on dehydration (Fig. 6). This magnetic switching is ascribed to the adsorption and desorption of water molecules at the CoII centres. Such processes change the coordination geometry at CoII from pseudo-octahedral to pseudo-tetrahedral, thus modulating the magnetic interactions from ferromagnetic coupling in the hydrated solid to antiferromagnetic.

In the two previous examples the insertion of solvent molecules into the framework has produced a profound change in the structure and in the properties of the MOF. Analogue reactions using other co-ligands also show similar 3D–2D conversion due to the insertion of water molecules into M–NC bonds, producing a shift in TC. Ohba, Kitagawa and co-workers81 have used N,N-dimethylethylenediamine (NNdmen) to prepare porous [Mn(NNdmenH)][Cr(CN)6], which shows a decrease in the ferrimagnetic ordering temperature from 60.4 K to 35.2 K upon hydration, whereas Inoue, Kurmoo and co-workers82 have used 1,2-propanediamine (pn) for the preparation of [Mn(rac-pnH)][Cr(CN)6], which shows a decrease in the ferrimagnetic ordering temperature from 70 K to 36 K upon hydration. Although dehydration processes are quite common, the examples here presented are reversible and occur without collapse of the structure.

A very interesting example that combines mainly all types of changes described above was recently reported by Pinkowicz, Sieklucka and co-workers.83 {[MnII(pydz)(H2O)2][MnII(H2O)2][NbIV(CN)8]·2H2O}n (pydz = pyridazine) is a 3D coordination polymer with a cyanido-bridged Mn2Nb skeleton consisting of a Mn–NC–Nb square-grid cross-linked at NbIV centers by Mn–NC–Nb ladders. The only terminal CN ligand of the [Nb(CN)8] moiety is involved in a hydrogen bond with one of the coordinated H2O molecules, whereas the noncoordinating nitrogen atom of the pyridazine ligand hydrogen bonds the other coordinated H2O molecule. Four water molecules per formula unit can be removed upon a few hours of dehydration, resulting in {[MnII(pydz)(H2O)][MnII(H2O)][NbIV(CN)8]}n. The loss of water permits a double topochemical reaction in which the terminal cyanide ligand and the noncoordinating nitrogen atom of the pydz ligand (which previously hydrogen bonded the water molecules) coordinate to Mn centres. In addition, the bond between the pydz ligand and Mn breaks. This change produces a change in the coordination spheres of the Mn centres from the original octahedral N3N′O2 and N4O2 to square-pyramidal geometry N4O and octahedral N4N′O respectively. Further dehydration produces the subsequent formation of the anhydrous phase {[MnII2(pydz)][NbIV(CN)8]}n, affecting only the coordination spheres of both Mn atoms (which are tetrahedral N4 and trigonal-bipyramidal N4N′). Interestingly, this two-step process is fully reversible. These structural changes cause an increase in the ordering temperature of the coordination polymer (43 K → 68 K →100 K) due to the increase in the connectivity of the magnetic centres and the reduced coordination numbers caused by the dehydration process (Fig. 7).

No significant structural changes. Although they might not be considered “dynamic MOFs”, there are also microporous framework materials where the guest sorption does not affect either the ligand or the coordination environment, but modification of the magnetic properties occurs.84–94 One example is the family of porous magnets [M3(HCOO)6], where M = Mn, Fe, Co, or Ni,89–94 recently discovered independently by three groups, which consists of a diamond net combining octahedral and tetrahedral nodes with the pores occupied by a great variety of solvent molecules, which can be removed upon heating. Despite the lack of changes in the framework upon guest sorption, these compounds exhibit ferromagnetic ordering with a guest-dependent critical temperature, which might be due to the modification of the host–guest hydrogen-bonding network.
2.1.2. Spin crossover frameworks. Dynamic porous materials based on spin-crossover complexes are very numerous.95 In fact, all spin crossover complexes could be considered as dynamic materials due to the expansion/shrinkage of the lattice generated by this phenomenon. The origin of these dynamics arises from the variation in the number of electrons in bonding and anti-bonding orbitals caused by the spin transition that modifies the metal–ligand distances, leading to an expansion/contraction of the framework. Temperature, light or pressure are common physical stimuli that produce this transition, but MOFs based on these molecular complexes can also exhibit magnetic switching via a chemical stimulus. This aspect has only been recently developed, although examples of spin-crossover MOFs were already known almost 20 years ago.96 Typically, the high sensitivity of these porous materials is evidenced by the modification of the transition temperature upon physisorption of specific molecules within the porous framework97–103 or even upon oxidative addition reactions following gas sorption at metal sites that are far from the magnetic centres.104,105 Here we will focus on the two main families of SCO porous MOFs.
Guest modulated spin-crossover. One of the first examples of modulation by guest sorption of the spin-crossover properties in porous materials was shown by Kepert and co-workers.97 The nanoporous metal–organic framework Fe2(azpy)4(NCS)4·(guest) (azpy = trans-4,4′-azopyridine) displays reversible uptake and release of guest molecules (MeOH, EtOH, and n-PrOH), which form hydrogen bonds with the framework. The framework structure displays a considerable flexibility with the guest uptake and release (Fig. 8), causing substantial changes in the local geometry around the FeII centres. Thus, the sorbed phases undergo “half-spin” crossovers, whereas the desorbed phases show no switching property. In the synthetic procedure, the azopyridine ligand can be replaced by different bis-pyridyl ligands producing the most extensive class of porous SCO materials,98–101 in which the desorption/adsorption of guest molecules into the 1-D channels produces the variation in SCO temperature as a consequence of the structural transformations associated with the guest sorption.

More recently, the effects of guest molecules in pillared Hofmann systems {Fe(pz)[M(CN)4]} (M = Ni,102 Pt103) have been reported (Fig. 9a). These systems exhibit hysteretic spin-crossover under ambient conditions. In two independent studies, Ohba et al. and Southon et al. have shown the stabilization of either the high spin (HS) or the low spin (LS) states of the porous framework upon sorption of guest molecules, achieving both guest-induced SCO and SCO-induced guest-exchange properties (Fig. 9b).

Spin-crossover induced by a change in the oxidation state of the metal. An interesting example in which a thermal treatment of a MOF induces a change in the oxidation state of the metal, which leads to a controlled modulation of the magnetic properties, has been found in the layered compound [CoIIILaIII-(notp)(H2O)4]·nH2O [where (notp)6− is the hexaanion of the 1,4,7-triazacyclononane-1,4,7-triyl-tris(methylenephosphonic acid]. This heterometallic coordination polymer has been reported by Bao et al.106 The diamagnetic compound is formed by binding an octahedral CoIII(notp)3− unit to La(H2O)43+ through phosphonate oxygen atoms. Upon heating, both the coordinated and non-coordinated water molecules can be removed, producing a change in the oxidation state of the cobalt (from CoIII to CoII) (Fig. 10). As a result, the spin state of the compound changes from a diamagnetic low-spin state (for CoIII) to a paramagnetic high-spin state (for CoII). This process is reversible on rehydration in air. The electron transfer is caused by the gradual release of the coordinated water molecules around the La atom. A combination of UV/Vis and EXAFS spectroscopies and DFT calculations was used to elucidate the origin of the change of the electronic structure of the cobalt centre, and it was found that the four vacant sites of the lanthanide ion upon removal of the water molecules are refilled by the neighbouring oxygen atoms of the adjacent Co(notp) moieties. This causes an increase in the Co–O bond lengths and a decrease in the Co–N bond lengths, changing ligand-field strength mediating the spin state of the Co ions.

Changes in the coordination environment of the SCO centres. Another possibility for tuning the spin-crossover properties consists of the modification of the coordination sphere of the SCO centres. This approach has been employed by Aromí, Gamez and co-workers, who have synthesized a linear coordination polymer of formula [Fe3(dpyatriz)2(CH3CH2CN)4(BF4)2](BF4)4·4CH3CH2CN with one-dimensional channels (Fig. 11a).107 These channels provide a path for the exchange of the coordinated propionitrile ligands. Thus, replacement with acetonitrile or 1-propanol was found to take place upon immersion into the corresponding solvent, with a decrease in the transition temperature from 300 K to 273 K (acetonitrile) or suppression of the spin-crossover properties, but maintaining the overall crystal structure (Fig. 11b). However, replacement with water molecules from the atmosphere results in a structural change (reversible upon immersion into propionitrile), most likely due to the different hydrogen bonding capabilities of the different ligands. This transformation involves suppression of the spin-crossover properties, presumably due to a reduction of the ligand field around the FeII.

A beautiful example reported by Real and co-workers shows the possibility of tuning the spin-crossover temperature using a topochemical reaction.108 [Fe(pmd)-(H2O){M(CN)2}2]·H2O (pmd = pyrimidine; M = Ag or Au) is a cyanide-based bimetallic 3D coordination polymer in which the uncoordinated nitrogen of the pmd ligand forms hydrogen bonds with coordinated H2O molecules of adjacent Fe centres, providing favourable conditions for a topochemical reaction to take place. Thus, upon heating, the coordinated water molecules are removed (in addition to the non-coordinated ones) with the subsequent coordination of the pmd ligand which now serves as a bridge between the two crystallographically independent Fe centres (Fig. 12a). Remarkably, this process is reversible upon exposure to an atmosphere of H2O. As a result of this structural transformation, the transition temperature is decreased by over 80 K and an increase in the hysteresis is also observed (Fig. 12b).

The possibility of synthesizing Hofmann chlatrates with Pt open metal sites permits us to tune the transition temperature of this type of dynamic porous coordination polymers by oxidative addition of halogens. Thus, upon exposure of {Fe(pz)[Pt(CN)4]} to dihalogens, an associative oxidation of PtII to PtIV occurs with a reduction of the dihalogen to the corresponding halide resulting in the formation of {Fe(pz)[Pt(CN)4(X)p]} [X = Cl− (p = 1), Br− (p = 1), I− (0 < p < 1)].104,105 This oxidative addition to the Pt centres modifies the σ-donor capability of the nitrogen atom of the Pt–CN group, provoking a change in TC. Thus, the more electronegative X induces a decrease in the ligand field, and so TC is decreased.

2.2. Non-porous MOFs

As seen in the previous section, porous coordination polymers have provided many examples in which the physisorption of guest molecules provoke subtle (or even profound) changes in the framework that affect their magnetic properties. However, the preparation of porous magnetic materials is not easy because magnetic coupling between spin carriers is weak in the porous network. Very recently we have shown that magnetic properties can also be tuned through gas uptake in the absence of pores.109 Since there is no possibility of a “guest molecule” being accommodated within pores, the gas molecules react with the material being therefore incorporated as part of the network. This results in significant chemical changes and atomic motions within the crystalline solid.

Crystalline [Cu(pyim)(Cl)(MeOH)] (pyimH = 2-(imidazol-2-yl)pyridine) displays a two-step irreversible–reversible chemisorption process, which causes substantial changes in the geometry and connectivity between the metal centres (Fig. 13), leading to three different compounds that exhibit distinct magnetic properties (Fig. 14). Thus, the as-synthesized material forms antiferromagnetic chains of pentacoordinated square pyramidal Cu centres. Upon chemisorption of 3 equivalents of HCl molecules the resultant ionic solid, (H2pyimH)[CuCl4], presents ferromagnetic interactions between the distorted tetrahedral [CuCl4]2− anions. Although this process is not fully reversible, this compound is able to extrude 2 equivalents of HCl yielding the molecular compound cis-[CuCl2(pyimH)] where the square planar Cu centres show antiferromagnetic interactions. The magneto-structural switching of these materials arises from the presence of labile copper(II) centres within the coordination network, which are capable of reacting with gaseous HCl or releasing HCl in a cyclic manner without loss of effectiveness. Importantly, these non-porous frameworks display a considerable flexibility with the guest uptake and release, which do not disrupt the crystallinity of the compounds. The structural analysis shows that three equivalents of HCl molecules are inserted into all Cu–N bonds requiring rupture of the covalent bond of the gaseous molecules (H–Cl) and of the coordination bonds of the polymeric compound in order that new N–H and Cu–Cl bonds can be formed. Thus, the coordination sphere of the Cu centres changes from three Cu–N, one Cu–Cl and one Cu–O bonds in the square pyramidal geometry to four Cu–Cl bonds in a distorted tetrahedral arrangement. The extrusion of HCl implies the elimination of two equivalents of HCl gas molecules resulting in the formation of a square planar Cu molecular complex with two Cu–N and two Cu–Cl bonds. Although the magnetic changes caused by the structural transformations in these compounds are only observable in the low temperature region (below 10 K) except for the pristine material, the substantial changes in coordination environments due to the sorption/desorption processes have permitted the use of electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectroscopy to monitor the reaction at room temperature due to its high sensitivity to the coordination environment of the metal centres.

Another possibility for tuning the magnetic properties in non-porous coordination polymers is by anion exchange. We have recently shown that a 3D coordination polymer, [Cu(btix)2(BF4)2]n (btix = 1,4-bis(triazol-1-ylmethyl)benzene) can reversibly exchange the coordinated BF4− anions by different anions with retention (PF6−, ClO4−) or modification (NO3−, Cl−) of the crystal structure, but maintaining the organic ligand as the only linker between the metal centers.110 However, an irreversible exchange occurs with N3− anions to generate a new coordination polymer, [Cu(btix)(N3)2]n, with a totally different connectivity between the CuII centres, which now involves both the organic and the inorganic ligands. During this process a drastic rearrangement of the network occurs due to the exchange of the BF4− anion by a N3− anion, accompanied by loss of one coordinated btix ligand, which is transferred to the aqueous solution. As expected, this structural transformation is accompanied with a change in the magnetic properties, which switches from antiferromagnetic in the pristine material to ferromagnetic in the azide exchanged system.

3. Physically-driven dynamic MOFs

Dynamic magnetic MOFs driven by a physical stimulus are scarce. A major source of such materials uses thermal heating to produce the structural change in the MOF. In this sense, some of the materials reported in the previous section are also thermally-driven, although the uptake/release of a physisorbed or a chemisorbed molecule is common to all of them. Furthermore, in these cases rigid ligands have been used, in such a way that a chemical stimulus has been able to cause minor changes in the conformation of the bridging ligands. In this section we will focus exclusively on those coordination polymers in which a physical stimulus is able to induce a dynamic change in the bridging ligands that are linking the metal ions. Two relevant examples will illustrate this possibility. In the first one, we will take advantage of the ability of some flexible ligands to undergo a syn–anti isomerism upon heating. In the second one, we will use the ability of the non-symmetric cyanide ligand to undergo a rotation induced by pressure when it is connecting FeII to CrIII in a Prussian blue analogue.

3.1. Syn–anti isomerism upon heating

Very recently, we have reported a family of dynamic coordination polymers with the flexible ligand 1,4-bis(triazol-1-ylmethyl)benzene (btix) where the ligand adopts a syn conformation, {[M(syn-btix)2(OH2)2]·2NO3·2H2O}n [M = Co, Zn].111 This framework consists of non-interpenetrated 1D cationic chains with large cavities that are partially filled with anions and solvent molecules. Upon heating, the chain compounds are able to extrude both coordinated and non-bonded water molecules, but also induces a conformational change in the flexible btix ligands from syn-conformation to anti-conformation, leading to a material of formula {[M(anti-btix)2(NO3)2]}n, which forms a 2D neutral grid with (4,4) topology (Fig. 15). This solid-state transformation requires a rearrangement in the metal environment and implies the cleavage of coordination bonds between MII ions and btix ligands, followed by the rotation of btix ligands through the methylene groups to adopt the anti conformation and subsequent re-formation of M–N bonds. Additionally, a ligand substitution takes place since the loss of coordinated water molecules is followed by coordination of the NO3− anions.

This structural transformation has been monitored through the use of magnetic techniques as it provokes changes in the magnetic properties of these magnetic MOFs. Thus, magnetic susceptibility measurements have been used to study the increase in the dimensionality of the network from 1D to 2D whereas EPR spectroscopy has been used to detect changes in the coordination sphere of the metal ion. The decrease in χMT is similar in both cases and indicates that the Co–Co exchange interactions are very weak in both compounds, being totally insensitive to the different conformations adopted by the bridging btix ligand (syn vs. anti) which cause a change in the dimensionality of the networks. EPR spectroscopy has shown to be very useful to detect the minor modifications that suffer the CoII coordination sphere upon transformation of the chains to the layered structure, and this transformation has been followed without detecting an intermediate in the reaction (Fig. 16). Hence, in this work it has been shown the possibility of using a magnetic technique as the EPR to monitor the structural changes induced in a dynamic MOF.

3.2. Linkage isomerism in Prussian blue analogues induced by pressure

The Prussian blue analogues (also referred to as bimetallic cyanide complexes) have afforded a rich variety of responsive molecule-based magnets, which can be very sensitive to an external stimulus.10 Since the pioneering work on the photoinduced magnetization of a cobalt–iron cyanide,112–117 reversible magnetic switching mediated by various physical and chemical stimuli, including visible light,118,119 X ray,120 pressure,121 temperature,122 electrochemical potential,123 or degree of solvation,124,125 has been observed. In all these examples, the switching mechanism has always involved an electron transfer as a key process. Hence, it occurs without a significant change in the crystal structure. Still, besides electron transfer, this class of compounds can undergo other internal chemical processes involving drastic changes in their structures, the most important of these being linkage isomerization.126 The influence of external physical stimuli on linkage isomerization processes is well-known in coordination chemistry,127,128 and several examples of bistability have been reported.129,130 Surprisingly, until very recently the possibility of tuning the magnetization of Prussian blue analogues via this structural rearrangement has not been explored, despite the fact that cyanide is a nonsymmetric ligand and linkage isomerization can be operative in these bimetallic systems. In this context, the first example showing the importance of linkage isomerism in the reversible switching of the magnetization induced by pressure was reported by our group in 2005 in polycrystalline samples of iron(II) hexacyanochromate.131

This molecular magnet, formulated as K0.4Fe4[Cr(CN)6]2.8·16H2O, crystallizes in the cubic Fmm space group and its structural features are typical of a Prussian blue analogue (Fig. 17). From the magnetic point of view, it behaves as a soft ferromagnet ordering at TC = 19 K. Upon application of external pressure (P) in the 0–1200 MPa range, the magnetization decreases considerably and the Curie temperature shifts to lower temperatures (Fig. 18). For example, at 1200 MPa, TC is 13 K and the saturation value of the magnetization (10 BM) is halved compared to that at ambient pressure (18 BM). The phenomenon is reversible: releasing the pressure results in a thermal dependence of the magnetization that matches that of the original sample (Fig. 18). An additional and irreversible decrease in the magnetization and ordering temperature (Tc = 8 K) is observed after prolonged treatment (21 days) at 1200 MPa. This piezomagnetic behaviour has been attributed to the linkage isomerism of the cyanide. In fact, under pressure some of the CrIII–CN–FeII linkages present in the compound isomerize to the more compressed CrIII–NC–FeII form, as demonstrated by pressure-dependent Raman measurements and also by X-ray absorption spectroscopy and magnetic circular dichroism (Scheme 1).132 This results in an increase in the ligand field around the iron(II) centres, which undergo spin crossover to the diamagnetic LS state. This molecular process explains that, as the pressure increases, the number of paramagnetic centres in the cubic lattice decreases, as do the net magnetization and the critical temperature of the material. At the local level, the rotational movement of the cyanide anions can be considered as a molecular switch of the magnetic interaction between nearest neighbours.

4. Concluding remarks

Coordination chemistry has permitted the preparation of crystalline materials with versatile structures and remarkable properties. Among these, the synthesis of dynamic materials has developed from simple applications (although very important) such as gas sorption or catalysis to post-synthetic modification capable of altering the networks in a pre-designed manner. Notice that this class of MOFs is not so common, as typically these coordination polymers are formed by rigid frameworks. Nevertheless, flexibility can also be found without bond cleavage in what is known as “breathing” MOFs, in which structural changes occur upon guest removal/inclusion.18,19

The possibilities opened by the presence of magnetic metal centres in these dynamic metal–organic materials have only been loosely investigated, despite the fact that magnetism is highly sensitive towards subtle changes in the connectivity and the environment. In this review we have focused on these dynamic magnetic MOFs. We have shown that both chemical and physical stimuli can be used to tune in a reversible manner the magnetic properties of the crystalline solid opening the way to develop applications as sensors and magnetic switches. We have also shown that these responsive materials can be used for molecular sensing applications even when they are not porous. This feature is very appealing when dealing with magnetic lattices as it allows overcoming the challenge of having large pores coexisting with cooperative properties. In this context we have also shown that magnetic cooperativity, often observed at very low temperature, is not always required to make these materials useful. In fact, the physical changes induced by a spin-crossover have shown to be much better suited to detect the structural changes occurring upon uptake/release of a guest molecule, as this phenomenon can be shifted to room temperature.

To conclude we should mention some of the current challenges and perspectives in the field of the dynamic magnetic MOFs. The first observation is that most of the reported examples of dynamic magnetic MOFs are chemically-responsive. In very few occasions a physical stimulus, such as light or pressure, has been used. This is related with the difficulty of switchable molecules to undergo a movement in the solid state. Soft crystals of low dimensionality, formed for example by magnetic layers connected by these molecules could be suitable in this context. A second point of future interest is that of using these physically-responsive materials as components of molecule-based spintronic devices. One can imagine for example the use of these hybrid materials as interfaces between a ferromagnetic electrode (acting as a spin injector) and an organic semiconductor layer (acting as a spin collector). By tuning the properties of this interfacial surMOF (surface-grown MOF)133,134 through a physical stimulus one can expect to tune the spin injection from the ferromagnetic electrode to the organic semiconductor and thus, the properties of the spin valve device. Interestingly, the possibility offered by these materials to accommodate molecules at the pores may provide in addition a way of increasing the interaction between the ferromagnet and the semiconducting molecules, as they can penetrate into the pores. Obviously, this is a challenging goal, which involves first the preparation of thin films of these magnetic MOFs and their deposition on various surfaces. Some initial steps along this line have already been done.135
