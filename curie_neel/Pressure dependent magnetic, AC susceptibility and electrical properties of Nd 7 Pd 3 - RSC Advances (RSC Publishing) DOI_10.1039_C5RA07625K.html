Pressure dependent magnetic, AC susceptibility and electrical properties of Nd7Pd3

Pramod Kumar*a, Puneet Jaina and Rachana Kumar*b
aMagnetic and Spintronic Laboratory, Indian Institute of Information Technology Allahabad, Allahabad 211012, India. E-mail: pkumar@iiita.ac.in
bNational Physical Laboratory, New Delhi 110012, India. E-mail: rachanak@nplindia.org

Received 27th April 2015 , Accepted 19th June 2015

First published on 19th June 2015

The effects of pressure on the magnetization and isothermal entropy change, AC susceptibility, and resistivity for Nd7Pd3 have been studied. Nd7Pd3 shows three magnetic transitions Tt (=15 K), TC (=34 K), and TN (=38 K) at a pressure of zero bar. TC and TN are very sensitive to the pressure and rates of change of TC and TN with respect to pressure, i.e., dTC/dp and dTN/dp are −1.906 K kbar−1 and −0.313 K kbar−1 respectively, whereas Tt is insensitive to pressure change. It has also been observed that the isothermal entropy change is also sensitive to pressure. Below 15 K, an indication of spin glass nature in the AC susceptibility data is seen. The time dependent magnetization at low temperatures also supports the spin glass nature, but at high temperatures it indicates a complex nature. The electrical resistivity data has been fitted to the relation ρ = ρ0 + AT2 in the temperature range 12–30 K and ρ = ρ0 + BT − CT3 in the temperature range 40–220 K. The M–H isotherm confirms the AFM nature of Nd7Pd3, which is confirmed further by the Arrott’s plot.

Introduction

The rare earth intermetallic compounds R7M3 (where R = rare earth metal and M = transition metal) have a hexagonal Th7Fe3-type structure (space group: P63mc).1 R in this structure occupies three non-equivalent sites (6c, 12d and 2b) whereas M resides at the site 12b.2 The compounds of this series have multiple magnetic transitions except for Gd7M3.5 As an example, Nd7Rh3 has two magnetic phase transitions at 34 K and 9.1 K,3 Sm7Rh3 has two transitions at 54 K and 25 K (ref. 4) and Gd7Rh3 behaves as an antiferromagnet below 150 K. In the same way, Nd7Pd3 transitions from behaving as an antiferromagnet to a paramagnet at around 38 K,6 and from a ferromagnet to an antiferromagnet at a temperature of 34 K.6,7

Unlike Nd7Rh3, which has the property of co-existing in antiferromagnetic and ‘super-cooled’ ferromagnetic phases after the reduction of the magnetic field to zero, and still does not show spin-glass anomalies,8 Nd7Pd3 shows spin-glass nature at low temperatures but does not co-exist in an antiferromagnetic and ‘super-cooled’ nature.3 In contrast to Gd7Pd3, which has spin dependent momentum density,9 Nd7Pd3 does not show this property, but Nd7Pd3 also has an interesting property that the FM → AFM (FM stands for ferromagnetism, AFM stands for antiferromagnetism), and AFM → PM (PM stands for paramagnetism) transition property of Nd7Pd3 is sensitive to pressure. Nd7Pd3 shows a strong pressure effect like that found in Ce7Ni3, where there is a transition from a magnetic state to a non-magnetic state that takes place at low temperatures by applying weak pressure.6,7 Rare earth based intermetallic compounds (R7M3) have applications in magnetostriction, magnetoresistance and magnetocaloric effects.10,11 The magnetocaloric effect (MCE), which manifests as the heating or cooling of magnetic materials due to a varying magnetic field, has attracted considerable attention. The origin of the MCE in many materials has been explained and its practical use to achieve low temperatures is being anticipated. The giant magnetocaloric effect exhibited by many rare earth (R)–transition metal (M) intermetallic compounds make them potential refrigerants for magnetic refrigerators.10,11 A large value of MCE spread over a wide temperature range is considered to be one of the most important requirements of a practical magnetic refrigerant system.10,11

In the present work, we calculated the pressure effect on temperature dependent magnetization, and also on isothermal entropy change. It was shown by Sengupta et al.,10 that in Nd7Pd3 there is an occurrence of kinetic hindrance, which leads to phase coexistence (supercooled ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic). They showed from the AC susceptibility data that such phase coexistence is different from spin. Nd7Pd3 shows spin glass nature, so we have measured AC susceptibility and temperature dependent normalized magnetization data to confirm the spin glass nature in our sample. In the present work, we have also calculated the M–H isotherms and Arrott’s plot to study the magnetic structure of Nd7Pd3. Electrical resistivity measurements were also performed to study the metallic nature of this compound.

Experimental details

The sample was prepared in an argon atmosphere using an arc melting furnace. The starting materials were of 99.8% purity. The ingots were checked by X-ray diffraction after annealing at 873 K for a week and found to be a single phase (using the fullProf software) of the Fe7Th3-type. The magnetization was measured at different applied pressures (P) using a Cu–Be clamp type cell, which can work at up to 12 kbar, attached to a SQUID magnetometer, whereas the AC susceptibility and transport were measured using a PPMS in the temperature range of 2–300 K. For the measurement of magnetization in a zero-field-cooled (ZFC) state, the sample was cooled from the paramagnetic state in zero applied field, and the magnetization was measured while warming the sample. The MCE has been calculated using M–H isotherms near the transition temperature. Before measurement at each temperature the specimen was zero-field cooled from 60 K.

Results and discussion

Fig. 1 shows the room temperature powder X-ray diffraction pattern and Rietveld refinement of Nd7Pd3. All peaks can be indexed as the hexagonal Fe7Th3-type cell in the space group P63mc (a = 10.1369(8) Å, b = 10.1369(8) Å, c = 6.38354(6) Å and γ = 120°). The sample was found to contain a small (2–3%) impurity of Nd3Pd2. The low values of RP and RB show that the pattern fitting is accurate12 (shown in Fig. 1). The Bragg peaks of Nd7Pd3 (green line) and Nd3Pd2 (violet line) are also shown in Fig. 1.

The calculated positions of the Bragg peaks are shown as vertical bars [Nd7Pd3 (green line) and Nd3Pd2 (violet line)] just below the plots of the observed and calculated intensities. Fig. 2 shows the temperature dependence of magnetization (M–T) at various applied pressures under field-cooled (FC), and zero-field-cooled (ZFC) modes, at 500 Oe. Fig. 2(a) shows the M–T plot for the FC condition. The dM/dT vs. T plot clearly indicates three magnetic transitions at 15 K (=Tt), 34 K (=TC) and 38 K (=TN) as reported by Hideoki et al.6 While cooling down the sample, we observe our first transition at around 38 K (at 0 bar), which is a paramagnetic (PM) → antiferromagnetic (AFM) transition6 and the next transition at around TC = 34 K (at 0 bar) is AFM to ferromagnetic (FM). Below this transition at 34 K, Nd7Pd3 shows ferromagnetism, and around Tt of 15 K Nd7Pd3 shows another transition, which is referred to as a glassy nature6 which is due to magnetic frustration. As is visible from Fig. 2(a), as the pressure is increased from 0 bar to 5 kbar (keeping H constant at 500 Oe), TC decreases from 34 K to 15 K, and at the highest pressure of 9 kbar, TN is dominant over TC and Tt. So we can say that at 9 kbar, there is only a PM → AFM transition, and there is no FM state.6 Fig. 2(a) clearly reveals that, as the pressure is increased from 0 bar to 9 kbar, TC and TN shift towards a lower value. This is shown graphically by Fig. 2(c) and (d), that with increasing pressure, TC and TN decrease at the rate of dTC/dp = −1.906 K kbar−1 and dTN/dp = −0.313 K kbar−1.6 We have not plotted dTt/dp as Tt is insensitive with regard to pressure. Fig. 2(b) shows the difference between ZFC and FC mode, and this difference decreases with increasing pressure. We also observed that with increasing pressure, the difference is reduced, the reason being that at high pressure TN dominates over TC and Tt.


Fig. 2 Magnetization vs. temperature data of Nd7Pd3 (a) under FC and (b) ZFC and FC conditions obtained at various external pressures and in an applied field of 500 Oe. (c) TC versus pressure and (d) TN versus pressure plots of Nd7Pd3 compounds.

A question arises of whether the magnetic state attained after reversing the field to zero is purely ferromagnetic in character. Fig. 3(a) shows the isothermal magnetization behaviour of Nd7Pd3 at 28 K, 36 K, 38 K, 40 K, and 50 K. It can be noted from Fig. 3(a) that, at low field, there is some magnetization in Nd7Pd3, and this magnetization attains a saturation moment of 1.8 μB per Nd3+.7 This property of attaining maximum saturation at a low magnetizing field indicates the ferromagnetic nature of Nd7Pd3, which is in agreement with Fig. 2(a). At high temperatures, there is a linear dependence of M on H, which accounts for the paramagnetic behaviour of Nd7Pd3 above the Neel temperature. Fig. 3(a) clearly reveals that as we go from 36 K to 38 K and then to 40 K, we obtain off-axis M–H plots, and off-axis plots are a characteristic of antiferromagnetic materials, so we concluded that Nd7Pd3 is moving towards antiferromagnetic behaviour from ferromagnetic behaviour. To determine the magnetic phase transition, we plotted the magnetization isotherms in the form of an Arrott plot.


Fig. 3 (a) M vs. H isotherms and (b) Arrott plots of Nd7Pd3 compound near ordering temperature. Before measurements at each temperature the specimen was zero-field cooled from 60 K.

However, Arrott plots can be used to estimate TN in antiferromagnetic materials, and TC in FM materials.14,15 In the case of ferromagnetic and paramagnetic substances, b is positive, while a is positive for paramagnetic and negative for ferromagnetic materials. Banerjee16 suggested that all Arrott plots with a positive slope give a second order magnetic phase transition while a negative slope gives a first order magnetic phase transition. A close view of Fig. 3(b) shows a negative slope at low temperatures, which indicates the first order magnetic phase transition at low temperatures. Also, at low temperatures we found that the Arrott plots are displaced to the left, which indicates the ferromagnetic behaviour. An S-shaped Arrott plot indicates a FOT, which is depicted in Fig. 3(b).

Fig. 4 shows the temperature variation of magnetization at different fields from 2 kOe to 50 kOe, with a gap of 2 kOe between each two successive fields, in ZFC mode. In Fig. 4(a) it is found that at a pressure of 0 bar, we observe a kink at around 15 K, and at 34 K, which matches with the data shown in Fig. 2(a) at 0 bar. Then, similarly to Fig. 2(a), we get a drop in magnetization at around 38 K (=TN). Fig. 4(a) also reveals that as the magnetic field is increased, TN increases. This same pattern is shown in Fig. 4(b)–(d), and as the pressure is increased, TN decreases.


Fig. 4 Temperature variation of the magnetization at different fields of (a) 0 bar, (b) 2 kbar, (c) 5 kbar and (d) 9 kbar.

Fig. 4(b) shows the temperature variation of magnetization at the same fields from 2 kOe to 50 kOe, but with a pressure of 2 kbar. So we can see here again Tt, TC and TN of 12 K, 28 K and 32 K; this data also coincides with the magnetization vs. temperature data in Fig. 2(a). The results for Fig. 4(c) and (d)are similar. We also note a pattern here, that as the pressure increases from 0 bar to 9 kbar, the magnetization decreases in value. At 9 kbar, the FM state is again absent as can be seen from Fig. 4(d). Fig. 4(d) also shows a huge broadening of the curves with increasing magnetic field; this is due to the crystal anisotropy of Nd7Pd3.

Fig. 5 shows the MCE variation in the compound Nd7Pd3 at various pressures. It can be seen from the figure that, with the increase in pressure, the peak in the ΔSM vs. T plot moves towards lower temperatures. We note that Nd7Pd3 exhibits a reversible magnetocaloric effect. The AC magnetic susceptibility is magnetic susceptibility which is obtained by the application of an AC magnetic field. AC susceptibility is written as

 
χac = χ′ − iχ′′ (3)

In eqn (3), χ′ and χ′′ refer to the real and imaginary components of the AC susceptibility. The real component is also called the in-phase component and the imaginary component is also called the out-of phase component. AC susceptibility is used to study the magnetic phase transition.17 From Fig. 6(a), we can see three transitions in the real component, where the first peak is around 15 K, which corresponds to the transition temperature, Tt, as shown in Fig. 2(a). Another similarity between Fig. 6(a) and 2(a) is that in Fig. 6(a) there is another peak at around 34 K. This can be viewed by taking Fig. 6(b) into consideration. If in the AC susceptibility of a sample, there is a peak in χ′ at a temperature, and for the same temperature there is a non-zero χ′′, then it means the sample is ferromagnetic. This is what is being indicated by Fig. 6(a) and (b), for the peak around 34 K, which is the TC, so we can say 34 K is the Curie temperature of the Nd7Pd3. Similarly we observe a peak in Fig. 6(a) at 38 K, which corresponds to the Neel temperature of Nd7Pd3. For a sample to be antiferromagnetic, there is a peak in χ′ at a temperature, and for the same temperature there is a zero χ′′. This is what is shown by Fig. 6(a) and (b), but as the frequency of the AC field increases, the AFM nature becomes reduced. In Fig. 6(b), there is a strong frequency dependence of AC susceptibility at low temperatures (i.e. below 15 K) which is a clear indication of a magnetic glassy state. The χ′′ vs. T plot shows that at Tf, there is a strong peak; at higher temperatures, χ′′ is nearly equal to zero, but it has a non-zero value below Tf. This is a characteristic of a spin glass transition.17 To understand the glassy nature at low temperatures, we analyzed the frequency dependent Vogel–Fulcher law and the time dependent magnetization fitting.


Fig. 6 Temperature variation of the (a) real and (b) imaginary components of the AC magnetic susceptibility of Nd7Pd3 in a 5 Oe AC magnetic field at various frequencies.

The glassy nature demonstrated in Fig. 6(b) is analyzed using the empirical Vogel–Fulcher (VF) power law and is shown in Fig. 7, which shows the variation of spin freezing temperature Tf with relaxation time T0. The AC susceptibility fits well with the power law

 
Tf = T0 + [(Ea/κB)/100]X (4)
where T0 is given by 1.54 K, Ea is the activation energy, κB is the Boltzmann constant, and Ea/κB = 14.95 K. As can be seen from Fig. 7, Tf increases linearly with an increase in the logarithmic plot of ω0/ω, where ω0 is the attempt frequency and ω is the operating frequency.


Fig. 7 Frequency dependent temperature data fitted using the empirical Vogel–Fulcher law below Tt.

Fig. 8, shows the normalized magnetization, M as a function of time at various temperatures in ZFC mode. M0 is the magnetization at t = 0. All the measured values of M are normalized with regard to M0.


Fig. 8 (a) Normalized magnetization vs. time (t) plot for the compound Nd7Pd3 measured at different temperatures in an applied field of 10 kOe in the initial H cycle. For each H, M(0) is the value of the magnetization recorded when the relaxation measurements were started. (b) Data at 3 K fitted with the equation M/M(0) = P1(−1 + 2tγ) + P2 × exp((−t/τ)β). (c) Data at 25 K fitted with the equation M/M(0) = −1 + 2tγ.

Fig. 8(b) shows the normalized magnetization varying as a function of time, at 3 K, and it has been found that at 3 K, the magnetization is explained using a combination of two laws named as the power law and stretched exponential law, given by

 
M(t)/M0 = P1(1 − 2tγ) + P2 × exp((−t/τ)β) (5)
Here, P1 and P2 are temperature dependent weight factors for the power law and stretched exponential, respectively. The stretched law is a characteristic associated with spin glass nature, so the stretched law in eqn (5) indicates the glassy nature of Nd7Pd3 at low temperatures. But as there is also a power law contribution in eqn (5), which signifies that glassy nature has not completely formed.

Fig. 8(c) shows that at 25 K, the normalized magnetization, M, is very well fitted with the power law

 
M/M(0) = −1 + 2tγ (6)

In Fig. 8(c) the red solid line is fit to the data using the power law.18 As at 25 K, the normalized magnetization obeys power law, which demonstrates the metastability across the FOT, similar to the solidification of a melt when the melt is undercooled. We are not able to fit eqn (6) to the other temperatures. This is a clear indication of complex magnetic structure at high temperatures.

To get a better insight into the magnetic state of the compound, measurements of electrical resistivity were carried out. Fig. 9 shows the electrical resistivity of Nd7Pd3 as a function of temperature. From Fig. 9(a), we found that the residual resistivity of the sample is about 0.01 mΩ cm. Nd7Pd3 shows a metallic character. There is a change in slope of the resistivity near all the magnetic transitions as shown in Fig. 2(a). Fig. 9(a) shows three transitions. It can be found that at low temperatures (i.e. temperatures below TC) resistivity follows the equation ρ = ρ0 + AT2 and at high temperatures (i.e. temperatures above TN) resistivity follows ρ = ρ0 + BT − CT3, where ρ0 is the residual resistivity9 given by 8.67 × 10−6 Ω cm at low temperatures and 6.91 × 10−5 Ω cm at high temperatures respectively. A, B and C are temperature independent constants.9,19 From the electrical resistivity, we can conclude that Nd7Pd3 is metallic in nature over the entire temperature range, and because of the loss of spin disorder scattering due to spontaneous magnetic ordering, there is a drop in resistivity at TC = 34 K.


Fig. 9 (a) Temperature dependence of the electrical resistivity of the compound Nd7Pd3. Electrical resistivity data fitted to the relation (b) ρ = ρ0 + AT2 in the temperature range 12–30 K (b) ρ = ρ0 + BT − CT3 in the temperature range 40–220 K.

The second term in ρ = ρ0 + AT2 is proportional to T2 and represents the lattice spin waves. For Fig. 9(b), A = 5.8 × 10−8 Ω cm K−2 and suggests that electron spin-wave scattering is the dominant factor that helps to determine electrical resistivity in the temperature range of 12–32 K. Similarly, in the high temperature range, for ρ = ρ0 + BT − CT3, B = 4.58 × 10−7 Ω cm K−1 and C = 2.37 × 10−12 Ω cm K−2, which indicates the phonon contribution and also indicates that s–d scattering9 is important in the high temperature range i.e. above 40 K. Fig. 9(b) and (c) are plotted to determine the dominant contributions in the electrical resistivity data.20

Conclusions

Electrical resistivity, AC susceptibility, MCE and magnetization measurements were performed on Nd7Pd3, showing three magnetic transitions, first at around 15 K, then second at around 34 K and the third at around 38 K, where 34 K and 38 K are the Curie temperature and Neel temperature of Nd7Pd3, respectively. For a field of 10 kOe, Nd7Pd3 showed a MCE of 3.8 J kg−1 K−1 at 0 bar. The calculated values of TC and TN match with the M–H isotherm, AC susceptibility and temperature dependent electrical resistivity as well. The spin glass nature has also been confirmed by the temperature dependent AC susceptibility, normalized time dependent magnetization and Vogel–Fulcher law. The χ′′ vs. T plot shows that at Tf, there is a strong peak; at higher temperatures, χ′′ is nearly equal to zero, but it has a non-zero value below Tf. This is a characteristic of a spin glass transition. The electrical resistivity of Nd7Pd3 as a function of temperature shows that Nd7Pd3 has a metallic nature over the entire temperature range, and because there is a loss of spin disorder scattering, there is a drop in resistivity at TC = 34 K. The M–H isotherm and Arrott’s plots show the AFM nature of Nd7Pd3, which is in accordance with the M–T data in FC mode. The M–T data at various pressures and at a constant field of 50 kOe confirms the magnetic anisotropy of Nd7Pd3. This data has been verified by the MCE plots. A large refrigerating cooling power, large isothermal entropy change, soft magnetic behaviour and wide operating temperature range make it an attractive candidate as a magnetic refrigerant in the low temperature region.
